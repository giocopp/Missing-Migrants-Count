Where do migrants die or go missing at sea — and how are they counted?

Counting deaths and disappearances at sea is inherently difficult and often depends on the testimonies of survivors from shipwrecks, or, when there are no survivors, on recovered bodies and thorough cross-referencing of all accessible information about the missing person. These numbers are rarely precise and should be interpreted cautiously; they almost certainly represent a lower bound of the actual total.

Giorgio Coppola 6 May 2026

Just since January 2026, more than 1,200 people have lost their lives or are missing in the Mediterranean Sea trying to reach Europe — what the International Organization for Migration (IOM) has called the deadliest start to a year since it began counting. Every day, bodies wash up on Italian beaches, along the Libyan coast, in the waters between the Greek islands, off the Atlantic coast of West Africa, or in the English Channel. Many others are never found: their boats sank before recovery, or vanished with no witness. The toll is rising even as landings have halved and interceptions by the so-called “Libyan Coast Guard” have dropped sharply: the chance of drowning on a single crossing has gone up.

The main global source is the IOM Missing Migrants Project, launched in 2014, which records people who die "in the process of migration towards an international destination," regardless of legal status. For each shipwreck the count combines confirmed dead — bodies recovered or deaths reported by a credible source — and the missing or presumed dead, derived from the estimated number on board minus survivors and recovered bodies. That estimated total — reconstructed from survivor testimony, distress calls, families and aerial imagery — is the hardest piece to pin down.

The official totals are best understood as documented minimums. Deaths go uncounted for many reasons: people vanish in "invisible shipwrecks" with no survivors, no distress call is made, bodies are never recovered, or the death falls before embarkation or after interception and is never linked to a route. The problem is particularly severe on the Central Mediterranean and Atlantic — the two deadliest migratory routes to Europe.

What IOM's data shows, is that since 2014, more than 40,000 people have died or gone missing on the five sea corridors plotted here. The Central Mediterranean alone accounts for more than two-thirds of that total. Yet several NGOs produce substantially higher tolls — they accept reports from the families of missing persons and run hotlines that reach migrants directly. On the Atlantic route alone, the Spanish collective Caminando Fronteras counted nearly eight times as many deaths and disappearances as IOM did in 2024 (Le Monde, Feb 2026).

Notice that IOM's record is hand-compiled — each verified incident typed (with an estimated lat/lon) into spreadsheets — so most coordinates are approximations, and a smaller set are demonstrably wrong, where the location described in an IOM row (say, "off Lesvos") and its lat/lon (the central Mediterranean, off Tunisia) point to different places. We draw those dots with a dashed red outline; the tooltip names the inconsistency.

As European policy has tightened — particularly the criminalisation of humanitarian rescue vessels in Italy — civil-society witnesses have been pushed out of operational areas, and more boats now disappear with no one there to document them. The dots on this map are an undercount we know to be incomplete; the silence in certain places is itself part of the story.

Data and methods

This piece draws on a single source — the IOM Missing Migrants Project — passed through a small R pipeline before the map renders it. The walkthrough below is in build order.

1. Source

Incident-level records from the IOM Missing Migrants Project, January 2014 through early May 2026. Each row in IOM's download describes one reported incident, with its own count of dead or missing attached. The current dataset covers 6,802 such rows for the five Europe-bound sea corridors over that period.

2. Scope: the five sea corridors to Europe

The map keeps only events tagged with one of the five sea corridors that connect to Europe:

  • Central Mediterranean — Libya / Tunisia → Italy / Malta
  • Western Mediterranean — Morocco / Algeria → Spain
  • Eastern Mediterranean — Türkiye → Greek islands / Cyprus
  • Western Africa / Atlantic route to the Canary Islands
  • English Channel — France → United Kingdom (IOM tag: Mainland Europe to the UK)

Land routes (Sahara desert crossings, the Türkiye–Europe land route, the Belarus–EU border, the Western Balkans, Italy–France, Ukraine to Europe) and post-arrival deaths inside Europe are excluded — they belong to a different story.

We also drop IOM's Cumulative Incident rows, which roll up multiple events into a single summary row; keeping them alongside the underlying Incident rows would double-count. Rows with missing or out-of-range coordinates (|lat| > 90, |lon| > 180) are dropped at the same step.

3. How incidents are geolocated

The IOM record is compiled by hand. A small team at IOM's Global Migration Data Analysis Centre (GMDAC) in Berlin reviews incoming reports — coast-guard logs, official records from medical examiners and sheriff's offices, IOM country offices, NGO reports, UNHCR briefings, social and traditional media, and survivor testimony collected by IOM field staff — verifies them against existing records to avoid double-counting, and types each verified entry into a single Excel-style spreadsheet that becomes the public CSV download. Each row carries the date, the death count, and a description of where the death occurred; the team then assigns a latitude and longitude to that description before publishing.

IOM is explicit that "in most cases these coordinates are only estimates," and the accuracy varies sharply with the source: a coroner autopsy on the US–Mexico border can yield exact coordinates, while a transliterated survivor testimony from North Africa is necessarily approximate, and accurate official coordinates are sometimes withheld for political reasons in sea-rescue cases (see IOM's methodology). Because the entries are typed in by hand into a spreadsheet, the same kinds of errors that haunt any manual data-entry process show up here too: a longitude entered with the wrong sign (a Libyan-coast death lands in the Atlantic), latitude and longitude accidentally swapped (a Mediterranean death lands in the Sahara), a placeholder pair (33.0148, 12.549) reused when the location was unknown, or coordinates copied from one row that drift onto a different one (so a death on the Aegean coast of Türkiye gets the central-Mediterranean coordinates from the row above). These are not flaws unique to IOM — they are the cost of running a manual, human-curated, single-spreadsheet operation as the only global open-access record of migrant deaths.

4. Coordinate cleanup and flagging

For each row we check whether the recorded coordinates fall within 500 km of any country mentioned in the row — the IOM-tagged Country of Incident, plus any other country named in the free-text location description (so "between North Africa and Italy" with country=Libya is checked against both Libya and Italy). The 500 km tolerance is loose enough to allow legitimate offshore sea deaths recorded 20–80 nautical miles out. When the coordinate is more than 500 km from every mentioned country, we test a few simple fixes — flipping the sign of the longitude, swapping latitude and longitude, or both — and accept a fix only when it brings the point clearly inside or alongside one of the mentioned countries (within 200 km). We don't drop the disputed rows. The deaths are real and they belong on the map; quietly removing them would mean the only thing visible would be the deaths IOM happened to geolocate cleanly. Instead:

  • Rows whose coordinates we could reconcile by a sign-flip or lat/lon swap are plotted at the corrected position and tagged corrected; the tooltip shows IOM's original coordinates so the change is visible.
  • Rows whose coordinates can't be reconciled are still plotted, at IOM's original coordinates, but tagged flagged: drawn with a dashed red outline and a tooltip warning that IOM's coords don't match any country mentioned in the row, so readers know the dot's position is the IOM-side error, not our reading of it.
  • Rows using the (33.0148, 12.549) "unknown location" placeholder, or where latitude exactly equals longitude (clearly garbage), are excluded — those aren't real coordinates that can be honestly displayed anywhere.

Every row we touched — rescued, flagged, or excluded — is logged with its original coordinates, country, route, location text and the fix that was applied in data/built/coord_audit.csv, so the IOM-side errors are auditable from this article.

5. Aggregation: one feature per event

Most events in the IOM record arrive as a single row with their own death count attached. But a non-trivial minority — about 220 events in our scope, around 3% of the total — arrive split across multiple rows that share the same coordinates and the same date, typically because individual deaths or sub-batches were reported separately (e.g., bodies recovered on different shifts, or the same shipwreck logged from more than one source). Plotting those as several overlapping circles would imply several separate events and obscure the actual scale of a single tragedy.

We therefore group rows that share a (latitude, longitude, date) triple and sum the death counts within each group, so one circle on the map equals one reported event regardless of how IOM split it across rows. For the ~96% of events that come as a single row, the aggregation is a no-op. Where a circle does aggregate more than one row, the tooltip says so — "N individual records collapsed into this event" — making the underlying structure visible to readers who want it.

6. Categorical harmonisation

IOM's Cause of death (category) and Region of origin fields use long, verbose labels (e.g., "Hazardous transportation methods", "Sickness / lack of access to adequate healthcare"). We collapse them into shorter buckets so the filter dropdowns stay scannable: cause of death → drowning, vehicle, violence, exposure, sickness, other; and region of origin → Sub-Saharan Africa, Northern Africa, Middle East / Levant, South / Central Asia, Other, Unknown.

7. Map rendering

The cleaned features are written to GeoJSON and rendered with Leaflet. Each circle is coloured by sea corridor and sized by the number of dead or missing (square-root scaling). The basemap is CartoDB Positron, chosen for its light grey palette with country and sea labels.

8. Migration-route overlay

Hand-digitized polylines layered under the death points trace six migration corridors: West Africa, Western / Central / Eastern Mediterranean, English Channel, and Other (Sahel and East-African feeders that don't terminate at a Mediterranean sea crossing, plus Northern and Eastern European onward routes). Each polyline is tagged major (thick) or minor (thinner) to distinguish dominant flows from feeder routes. Twelve main migration hubs are marked as open circles, and four sea-route arrival points (Lampedusa, Lesbos, Canary Islands, Almería) as small filled dots.

The geometry is approximate — real journeys are individual and varied. The shapes are illustrative context for where deaths cluster, not a precise map of any one person's journey. They were traced from IOM's Global Overview of Migration Routes portal and sanity-checked against IOM DTM displacement data at country and state level — confirming each corridor passes through actively displacement-affected geography. DTM does not publish corridor shapes, so the lines themselves remain hand-drawn; the cross-check validates the countries and regions, not the exact lines.

Further reading on these corridors: IOM DTM Global Overview of Migration Routes, Mixed Migration Centre, Frontex, UNHCR Refugee Data Finder.
